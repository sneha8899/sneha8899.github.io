Student Intern, Department of Biomedical Informatics

May 2018 - Present

I spent my last summer researching in the Department of Biomedical Informatics at The Ohio State University. In this role, I worked full time in a lab group of 10 people. Over the course of my time spent here, I've worked through several different projects and problems.

Before I could do any of this work, I had to first learn the programming languages used. This meant that I had to learn Python, SQL, and R, to do the data manipulation needed; so I spent the first few weeks primarily teaching myself Python. It was a hard experience, but I also think it was really worthwhile. That was a new experience for me, and now I feel confident in my ability to learn new languages both quickly and efficiently. During the summer, I continued to learn, and eventually taught myself SQL, PHP, and R.

My first task was to sort through several different databases and pull out the information that I needed to work with. In the end, I had to work with eight total databases, and I needed information about diseases, genes, and treatments. I had to keep all of them in order according to the database they came from. Additionally, all of this information had to be organized and standardized. With each set of information (disease, genes, treatments) I had to standardize it according to nationally recognized databases.

After I completed this, I had to combine all of the information into one large dataset. This was then organized into a few related tables in MySQL. Now, I am in the process of creating a website where all of this is publicly accessible. I am using a PHP backend to create a database-driven website. Users will be able to come to my website and query their results by any type of information (genes, variations, drugs, diseases) and view all related information. This will also habe a "guessing" setting, where the relationships shown are more indirect. This project culminated in a poster, which can be seen on the side.

In addition to my own use, all of the information that I parsed and the codes I used to do it had to be clear and comprehensible by all other members of my lab. As the sole non-Chinese speaker, I had to really be careful in the way that I coded and displayed my results.

I also worked with a few other members on their projects, which involved different analyses of similar data. Again, several of these projects actually used the original data that I had generated. Additionally, an older member of the lab was working on his PhD project, which was to create a deep learning model that could help predict a specific type of cancer within a person based on their gene expression data. This was an interesting project because it was really unlike anything that I had done before, and I had to teach myself how to work with it. This is something that I am still in the process of working on.
