Student Intern, Department of Biomedical Informatics

May 2018 - Present

I spent my last summer researching in the Department of Biomedical Informatics at The Ohio State University. In this role, I worked full time in a lab group of 10 people. Over the course of my time spent here, I've worked through several different projects and problems.

Before I could do any of this work, I had to first learn the programming languages used. This meant that I had to learn Python, SQL, and R, to do the data manipulation needed; so I spent the first few weeks primarily teaching myself Python. It was a hard experience, but I also think it was really worthwhile. That was a new experience for me, and now I feel confident in my ability to learn new languages both quickly and efficiently. During the summer, I continued to learn, and eventually taught myself SQL, PHP, and R.

My first task was to sort through several different databases and pull out the information that I needed to work with. In the end, I had to work with eight total databases, and I needed information about diseases, genes, and treatments. I had to keep all of them in order according to the database they came from. Additionally, all of this information had to be organized and standardized. With each set of information (disease, genes, treatments) I had to standardize it according to nationally recognized databases.

After I completed this, I had to combine all of the information into one large dataset. This was then organized into a few related tables in MySQL. Now, I am in the process of creating a website where all of this is publicly accessible. I am using a PHP backend to create a database-driven website. Users will be able to come to my website and query their results by any type of information (genes, variations, drugs, diseases) and view all related information. This will also habe a "guessing" setting, where the relationships shown are more indirect. This project culminated in a poster, which can be seen on the side.

In addition to my own use, all of the information that I parsed and the codes I used to do it had to be clear and comprehensible by all other members of my lab. As the sole non-Chinese speaker, I had to really be careful in the way that I coded and displayed my results.

I also worked with a few other members on their projects, which involved different analyses of similar data. Again, several of these projects actually used the original data that I had generated. Additionally, an older member of the lab was working on his PhD project, which was to create a deep learning model that could help predict a specific type of cancer within a person based on their gene expression data. This was an interesting project because it was really unlike anything that I had done before, and I had to teach myself how to work with it. This is something that I am still in the process of working on.

The Robot Project

January 2018 - April 2018

When I look back at the second semester of my freshman year of college, the robot project is really what comes to mind. This was a semester long project to design, build, program, and compete a fully autonomous robot. There were several tasks that were required to be completed (and would impact our grades), and several that were just up for bonus points. The course itself consisted of two levels, with two ramps to get up and down.

My team consisted of four different people, and we all had our respective strengths - some in building, some in programming, and some in documentation. To the side there are pictures of our robot as it went through its different designs, all the way until the final. We also had to program the robot so that it could autonomously complete the course. This was done in C++, and the "brain" of the robot was the FEH PROTEUS, a touchscreen microcontroller specifically created for use in Ohio State's Honors Engineering Program.

An important aspect of this project was attention to detail. Like I said, there were several different required tasks, and these were scattered throughout the course. When programming the robot, we had to make sure that we could complete those objectives. Every task was dependent on where the robot started or ended, which meant that consistency was key. This led to an implementation of a PID (Proportional, Integral, Derivative) motor control function. Essentially, this function accounted for any sort of drag or wheel obstruction that might occur while the robot is moving. This enabled us to have more consistent, accurate runs.

The Software Design Project

December 2017

At the end of my first semester of college, a team of three people - myself and two others - were tasked with programming a game on the FEH PROTEUS. Again, the PROTEUS is a touchscreen microcontroller. This was my first real experience with creating something of this magnitude, and it was also in a relatively new language to me: C++. My group decided to create a version of the classic game FlappyBird.

This was a challenging task for a few reasons. FlappyBird consists of several different parts: the top and bottom obstacles (which move horizontally across the screen) and the bird (which moves horizontally). My group had to determine how we could do that efficiently. We also needed to take in user input, a tap to the screen, so that the bird could move up and down and so the user could select from a start menu. Finally, we had to consider difficulty and the speed of the game; the original sped up as time went on, and that was something that we wanted to do as well.

We ended up creating several functions that could accomplish this for different objects. Both the top and bottom obstacles moved the same way, so we created one general Obstacle_Move function. The bird moved up and down, so we created a separate one for that. This is an example of the foundation of the way that I try and code. Creating multipurpose aspects makes code a lot more efficient and comprehendible.
